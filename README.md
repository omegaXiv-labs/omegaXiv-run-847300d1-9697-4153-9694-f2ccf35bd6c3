Title: Glucose-Responsive Insulin Design via Machine Learning and Molecular Binding Modeling

Abstract:
Type 1 diabetes requires lifelong insulin replacement because pancreatic β-cells cannot regulate glucose levels. Current insulin therapies deliver fixed pharmacokinetic responses and cannot dynamically adapt to changing blood glucose, which exposes patients to both hypoglycemia and hyperglycemia. A long-standing objective in diabetes research is therefore the development of glucose-responsive insulin (GRI) whose activity increases when glucose levels rise and attenuates when glucose falls.

Recent advances have demonstrated partial feasibility of this concept. Several approaches have been explored, including glucose-binding polymers, phenylboronic-acid-based systems, glucose-oxidase responsive delivery materials, and engineered insulin analogs whose activity depends on glucose-dependent interactions. In 2024, a molecular insulin analog reported by Novo Nordisk showed glucose-dependent bioactivity within a physiologically relevant glucose range in vivo, representing the most convincing demonstration to date that insulin itself can be engineered to exhibit glucose-modulated activity. Despite these advances, glucose-responsive insulin remains an open scientific and translational challenge because candidate systems often suffer from instability, weak specificity, or insufficient modulation of insulin receptor activation.

This project frames the discovery of glucose-responsive insulin as a computational molecular binding problem in which machine learning and bioinformatics models are used to predict how insulin variants or insulin-binding complexes change their functional activity in the presence of glucose. The objective is to identify molecular designs in which glucose concentration modulates insulin receptor accessibility or binding affinity, producing an intrinsic feedback mechanism that stabilizes blood glucose levels.
